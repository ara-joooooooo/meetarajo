Syracuse Crime Has Dropped Dramatically — But Some Challenges Remain

By Ara Jo | JNL 221, Newhouse School | May 2026

Syracuse, New York has undergone a dramatic transformation in public safety over the past three decades. According to data from the New York State Division of Criminal Justice Services, total reported crimes in the city have fallen by nearly 43% since peaking at 12,706 incidents in 1991. By 2024, that number stood at 7,259 — a significant milestone for a city that has long grappled with some of the highest crime rates among similarly sized American cities.

The decline did not happen overnight. Throughout the 1990s, Syracuse consistently recorded more than 10,000 index crimes per year. The early 1990s were particularly difficult — 1991 saw the highest recorded total in the dataset at 12,706 incidents, followed closely by 11,864 in 1992 and 11,115 in 1993. These years represented a period of intense strain on the city, reflecting national trends of rising urban crime that affected cities across the United States.

The numbers began to fall more sharply in the early 2000s, dropping from 9,792 in 2002 to 7,983 by 2004. That downward trend continued steadily through the following decade, reaching a modern low of 4,736 incidents in 2021 — the lowest total in the entire dataset going back to 1990.

But the story does not end there. Since 2021, crime has climbed back up, rising to 6,668 in 2023 and 7,259 in 2024. That three-year increase has raised questions about whether the city's hard-won progress is beginning to reverse — and what factors might be driving the change.

Sharon Grant, 71, who worked more than 15 years in the Onondaga County District Attorney's Office before returning to Syracuse, said the recent rise in thefts and assaults has become increasingly noticeable. "During my time in the District Attorney's Office, crime steadily declined because of stronger enforcement, community partnerships and a focus on repeat offenders," she said. "But recently, the increase in thefts and assaults has become hard for residents to ignore."

Property Crime Dominates the Numbers

Property crime remains by far the dominant category of criminal activity in Syracuse. Of the 7,259 total crimes reported in 2024, larceny — the theft of personal property — accounted for 3,831 incidents, making up more than half of all reported crime in the city. Motor vehicle theft followed with 1,270 cases, a figure that reflects a national trend of rising car thefts that accelerated during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. Burglary accounted for another 1,047 incidents.

Violent crime, while far less common than property crime, remains a serious concern. In 2024, Syracuse recorded 810 aggravated assaults, 195 robberies, 85 rapes and 21 murders. While these numbers represent a fraction of total crime, they carry an outsized impact on communities and public perception of safety.

Poverty and Economics Play a Role

Researchers who study urban crime consistently point to economic conditions as one of the strongest predictors of crime rates. Syracuse has a poverty rate of approximately 30% — nearly double the national average of around 15%. High poverty, limited employment opportunities and concentrated disadvantage in certain neighborhoods all create conditions that researchers associate with elevated crime.

Syracuse is not alone in experiencing these patterns. Many mid-sized post-industrial American cities — including Rochester, Buffalo and Utica — have faced similar challenges over the same period. All of these cities saw significant crime reductions through the 1990s and 2000s, followed by more complicated trends in the years since the pandemic.

A Regional Contrast

The data also reveals an important distinction between how crime is distributed across different parts of the Onondaga County region. While Syracuse City Police recorded 7,259 total incidents in 2024, neighboring agencies tell a very different story. North Syracuse Village Police reported just 100 total crimes that same year. The contrast underscores how concentrated urban crime tends to be — and how dramatically conditions can differ within just a few miles.

For residents of Syracuse, these numbers are more than statistics. They represent daily decisions about where to walk, where to park, and whether to feel safe in their own neighborhoods. Community organizations, city officials and law enforcement agencies have all invested in efforts to address the root causes of crime — from youth programs and job training to increased police presence in high-crime areas.

Tyreeka Thomas, 47, a lifelong Syracuse resident, said the recent spike in crime has become more visible in everyday life. "People are noticing more thefts, assaults and stolen vehicles than they did a few years ago," she said. "A lot of cities are seeing the same thing after the pandemic, especially in communities struggling with poverty and fewer opportunities for young people."

Whether the recent uptick proves to be a temporary blip or the beginning of a longer reversal remains to be seen. What the data makes clear is that Syracuse has come a long way — and that sustaining that progress will require continued attention, investment and community effort for years to come.
